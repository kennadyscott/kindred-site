The conversations you're dreading

Relationships are made of conversations — including the hard ones. Three small scripts for saying the thing.

How to say "I'm not okay"
  • Pick the person, then the moment. Low stakes, side by side — car rides and walks count. Eye contact is optional.
  • Lower the bar. You don't need the right words. "I've been struggling more than I've let on" is a complete sentence.
  • Tell them what you need from them. To listen? To distract you? To help you find help? People do better with a job.
  • Let it be awkward. Connection survives awkward. It rarely survives silence.

How to set a boundary without a speech
  • One sentence beats a paragraph. The longer the speech, the more it sounds like an opening offer.
  • Boundaries are about your behavior, not theirs. "I'm going to head out at eight" lands better than "you always keep me too late."
  • Expect the wobble. Feeling guilty afterward doesn't mean you were wrong. It usually means the boundary was new.
  • Repeat calmly. Don't relitigate. A boundary restated is a boundary; a boundary debated is a negotiation.

How to actually apologize
  • Name the specific thing. "I'm sorry I dismissed you in front of everyone" repairs more than "I'm sorry you're upset."
  • Skip the "but." Everything before the "but" gets deleted by everything after it.
  • Ask what would help. Repair is theirs to define, too.
  • Change is the apology. The words open the door. Behavior is what walks through it.
