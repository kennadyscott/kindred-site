What therapy is actually like

Less mysterious than the movies. More useful, too.

1

The first session

Mostly, you talk. They ask questions. No couch required, no childhood inventory on day one — and you're allowed to open with "I don't know where to start."

2

Finding a rhythm

Usually weekly at first, around 50 minutes. The early sessions are about being understood, not being fixed.

3

The middle

Where the work happens. Some sessions feel lighter, some heavier — if it ever feels worse before better, say so. That's data, not failure.

4

Seasons and endings

Therapy isn't forever. People pause, finish, and come back years later. A good ending is part of the work, not an escape from it.

Types of therapy, translated

The acronyms are less important than they look. Here's what they actually mean.

CBT

Cognitive behavioral therapy

Catch the automatic thoughts that run the show, and practice new responses.

Often used for: anxiety, depression, habits

ACT

Acceptance & commitment therapy

Stop wrestling every thought. Make room for the hard ones, and move toward what matters anyway.

Often used for: anxiety, chronic stress, stuckness

EMDR

Eye movement desensitization & reprocessing

Help the brain re-file memories that stayed "live" — so the past stops firing like the present.

Often used for: trauma, PTSD

Psychodynamic

Insight-oriented therapy

Understand the patterns your past is still writing — especially the ones that keep repeating in relationships.

Often used for: relationship patterns, identity, depth work

IFS

Internal family systems

Meet the parts of you that pull in different directions — the critic, the protector, the one who's tired.

Often used for: self-criticism, trauma, inner conflict

Somatic

Body-based therapy

Work with the body, where stress actually lives — not just the story about it.

Often used for: trauma, anxiety, chronic tension

Here's the part directories don't tell you: research consistently finds the relationship with your therapist predicts outcomes better than the acronym does. The letters matter less than the fit.

The practical questions

How much does it cost?

In the U.S., typically $100–250 per session without insurance — but many therapists offer sliding-scale spots, and asking about cost is a completely normal first question, not a faux pas. Community clinics and training centers can be far less.

Does insurance cover it?

Often, yes — through in-network therapists, or "superbills" you submit for partial reimbursement. Kindred lets you filter by insurance up front, so coverage is a starting condition, not a surprise.

How long until it helps?

Many people feel some relief within the first few sessions — being genuinely heard does that. Meaningful change more often takes a few months of steady work. Anyone promising a fixed timeline is guessing.

Online or in person?

Research finds both work. Online removes commutes and widens your options; some people think better in a room that isn't their apartment. It's preference, not compromise — and you can filter for either.

What if I don't click with my therapist?

Then say so, or switch — therapists expect this, and the good ones want you to find your person even if it isn't them. Since fit predicts outcomes, switching isn't quitting. It's the method.

Is it confidential?

Yes, with narrow legal exceptions around safety — yours or someone else's — which every therapist explains in the first session, before you share anything.

The quiet worries

The questions people don't say out loud.

"What if I cry?"

People do. It's allowed. There's a reason the tissues are already on the table.

"What if I don't know what to talk about?"

"I don't know what to talk about" is a fine opening line. It usually isn't true for long.

"What if my problems aren't big enough?"

There's no minimum. If it's taking up room in your life, it qualifies.

"What if they judge me?"

You'd be surprised how little shocks a therapist — and how much relief there is in being met without flinching.

"What if it doesn't work?"

Sometimes a match doesn't. That's information about the fit, not a verdict on you — and it's exactly the problem Kindred was built to fix.
